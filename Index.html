The Discovery

A world unlike anything we expected.

01
Permanent magma ocean
Unlike any known rocky body, L98-59d maintains a global magma ocean over geological timescales — continuously feeding sulfur compounds into its atmosphere via volcanic outgassing.
02
Secondary atmosphere confirmed
JWST's NIRSpec G395H mode detected a strong absorption feature between 3.3–4.8 microns, deviating from a flat line by 2.6 to 5.6σ — ruling out stellar contamination as the source.
03
Neither a gas dwarf nor a water world
L98-59d breaks both dominant formation models. Its chemically reducing mantle and early sulfur/hydrogen content (>1.8% by mass) point to an entirely new planetary archetype.
04
Closest analogous super-Earth to date
At 34.6 light-years, L98-59d is one of the nearest confirmed super-Earths with detectable atmospheric chemistry — making it the prime candidate for follow-up observations.

Atmospheric Science

A world of sulfur and living rock.

L98-59d's atmosphere is not a relic — it is actively produced. A permanent magma ocean on the planet's surface continuously outgasses sulfur compounds, which then undergo photochemical reactions in the upper atmosphere to produce sulfur dioxide (SO₂) as the dominant absorber.

"While cooling and escape shrank this planet, a permanent magma ocean supplies sulfur to its atmosphere, in which SO₂ is produced by photochemistry."

— Nature Astronomy, March 2026 · Volatile-rich evolution of molten super-Earth L 98-59 d

This mechanism — a geologically active interior driving an evolving secondary atmosphere — was not predicted by existing planetary formation models. It positions L98-59d as a new reference point for understanding how small rocky planets retain and cycle volatiles over billions of years.
